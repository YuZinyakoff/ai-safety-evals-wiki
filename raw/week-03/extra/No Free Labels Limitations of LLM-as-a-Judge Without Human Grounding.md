###### Abstract

Reliable evaluation of large language models (LLMs) is critical as their deployment rapidly expands, particularly in high-stakes domains. The LLM-as-a-Judge framework, which uses prompted LLMs to evaluate response quality, is appealing due to its scalability, low cost, and strong correlations with human stylistic preferences. However, it remains unclear how accurately these methods can assess response quality when correctness matters more than style. To address this gap, we introduce the Business and Finance Fundamentals Benchmark (BFF-Bench), a dataset of 160 challenging questions and long-form responses authored by financial professionals. These experts subsequently evaluated the correctness of 1,200 responses generated by a diverse set of LLMs on both BFF-Bench and a challenging subset of MT-Bench. With this expert-annotated dataset of judgments (VERDICTS), we analyze the agreement between a suite of automated grading methods and human experts. While we observe that LLM Judges are more reliable than other grading methods, our findings reveal a clear pattern in LLM Judge performance: when not provided with a correct reference, judges show high agreement with human experts *only on questions the judges were able to correctly answer themselves*. We demonstrate that providing the judges with expert-written references largely mitigates this issue, highlighting the limits of using LLM-as-a-Judge without any form of human verification.

## 1 Introduction

For text generation tasks such as translation, summarization, and question answering, models provide free-form responses that are evaluated for some notion of overall quality [^8]. Because of the open-ended nature of the output space, automated evaluation methods often rely on lexical overlap heuristics that compare model outputs to trusted references (e.g., BLEU, [^42]; ROUGE, [^31]; and METEOR, [^6]) or—more recently—on methods such as reward models [^40] [^22] [^16] and LLM-as-a-Judge [^64] [^27]. Using LLM-as-a-Judge for evaluation has become increasingly prominent due to its flexibility and high correlation with human preference. Benchmarks such as MT-Bench [^64], AlpacaEval2.0 [^14], ArenaHardAuto [^28], and WildBench [^30] are commonly used to evaluate the capabilities of frontier language models.

Figure 1: LLM Judge agreement with human annotators for the pairwise correctness judgment task. The left plot shows agreement when using a LLM as a judge on questions it is able to answer correctly, whereas the right plot shows agreement on questions it answers incorrectly. None means the judge LLM is provided with no reference response, Self means the judge LLM is provided an unverified reference generated by the judge model itself, and Human means the judge LLM is provided a human-written gold (correct) reference. When judge LLMs are not provided a correct reference, their agreement with human judgments decreases substantially for responses to questions they cannot correctly answer. However, providing a correct reference recovers much of this agreement.

The LLM-as-a-Judge framework is typically used in the following way: a model that we want to evaluate (the candidate model) generates responses to a set of benchmark questions. Then, to evaluate these responses, an LLM (the judge model or LLM Judge) is prompted to produce some kind of quality score from each response. The candidate model’s response can be graded in isolation (single grading) or in comparison to an additional candidate model’s response (pairwise grading). Each of the aforementioned benchmarks validates their use of LLM-as-a-Judge by measuring correlations with human preference annotations. Human preferences can naturally vary along many different axes, making it difficult to determine exactly why one response might be preferred to another [^46] [^15] [^47].

In some high-stakes domains such as business and finance, however, correctness outweighs other attributes [^7] [^15] [^24]. In these settings, accurately judging response quality hinges on identifying whether a response is correct, which itself can require complex reasoning and domain knowledge [^49] [^64]. In this work, we investigate the following question: *Can LLMs make reliable judgments of correctness, and therefore quality, in these high-stakes domains?*

Our main contributions are as follows:

1. We create BFF-Bench: a new, challenging conversational benchmark with a focus on correctness This business and finance themed benchmark contains 80 conversations spanning 160 questions with long-form reference answers created and verified by domain experts.
2. Using data from BFF-Bench and MT-Bench, we create the Verified Expert Response Dataset for Identifying Correctness over Text Style, or VERDICTS: a dataset of expert judgments of correctness for 1,200 LLM responses to evaluate the reliability of methods of pairwise and single grading.
3. We demonstrate that on VERDICTS, whether an LLM Judge agrees with human experts is strongly related to its ability to answer the underlying benchmark question and the correctness of the provided reference (Figure˜1).

## 2 Related Work

Limitations of LLM-as-a-Judge. A growing body of work evaluates the shortcomings of LLM-as-a-Judge. Factors such as length, order, and tone of a response have all been shown to bias LLM Judges’ evaluation [^64] [^23] [^58]. Benchmarks specifically designed to reveal such shortcomings have emphasized the challenges of developing reliable LLM Judges [^23] [^61]. To mitigate such biases and enhance agreement with human annotators, various strategies have been proposed, including response-order randomization [^64], explicit debiasing against response length [^14], ensemble-based “jury” models [^52], rubric-driven evaluation frameworks [^18] [^33], prompt optimization techniques [^65], pairwise preference optimization methods [^34], and dynamically generated reference responses [^63].

LLM-as-a-Judge and Correctness. Prior works have investigated the ability of LLMs to generally evaluate correctness or factuality [^37] [^45] [^10]. Recent studies [^51] [^4] [^19] have demonstrated that LLMs can reach high agreement with human annotators when grading the correctness of responses to short-answer questions on existing datasets. [^15] specifically study how well standard LLM-as-a-Judge pipelines capture correctness and find that judge models often ignore response correctness in their quality assessments. [^49] constructed a pairwise correctness judgment benchmark out of existing datasets, also finding poor performance.

Expert-Informed LLM-as-a-Judge. Many datasets incorporate human knowledge with verified reference responses for LLM evaluation [^5] [^35] [^9] [^2]. [^44] introduce a collection of five datasets with domain-specific questions, expert-verified long-form solutions, and annotated reasoning errors. [^48] examines LLM-as-a-judge in the domains of dietetics and mental health by comparing GPT-4’s ratings to those of domain professionals, finding only moderate agreement on a dataset of 25 questions. In contrast, VERDICTS is the only dataset to contain questions, long-form answers, and judgments of correctness, all provided by the same domain experts.

## 3 Datasets

To use as little synthetic data as possible in constructing our core benchmarks, we worked with human experts to create challenging questions with reference solutions, and annotate response correctness. A more detailed description of our annotators and annotation process can be found in Appendix˜A. We introduce a novel dataset (BFF-Bench), a corrected subset of an existing dataset ((C)MT-Bench), and a set of human expert labels of model response correctness over these two datasets (VERDICTS).<sup>1</sup>

### 3.1 BFF-Bench

Our goal was to collect a benchmark that was conversational in nature but with unambiguous correctness. To have the same expertise available for both question creation and grading, we recruited a team of experts within a single high-stakes domain: business and finance. Although this limited the diversity of the underlying dataset, this tradeoff was necessary to scale both dataset creation and response grading.

We collected 80 two-turn conversational questions with gold reference answers, all written entirely from scratch by domain-experts. These questions are designed to be challenging and unambiguous, integrating both business and financial domain knowledge with quantitative reasoning. A sample question and reference answer are shown in Figure˜2.

The question-response pairs were developed by a team of financial professionals. Each pair underwent an iterative quality control process and verified. Questions were designed to have unambiguous answers to facilitate unambiguous correctness judgments. Many require mathematical manipulation, from simple arithmetic to the application of financial formulas, whereas others test domain knowledge. Given their intended difficulty and technical scope, each question-response pair required approximately one hour to create, clean, and verify.

### 3.2 (C)MT-Bench

To increase the diversity of our judgment set, we collected data from the math and reasoning subsets (40 total questions) from MT-Bench. This subset, unlike MT-Bench’s more open-ended categories,<sup>2</sup> is evaluated with references by default in the MT-Bench pipeline and leads to responses that tend to be unambiguously correct or incorrect.

Inspired by [^21], we reviewed the provided GPT-4-generated reference answers for these 40 questions and *found that 15 (37.5%) were either incorrect or inconsistent*.<sup>3</sup> An example of one of these errors in provided in Figure˜5 in Appendix˜C. We created correct references for each question by manually re-writing and verifying the existing references. These corrected references include the correct answer and a coherent reasoning chain, detailing all necessary sub-steps. We refer to this corrected subset as (C)MT-Bench.

### 3.3 VERDICTS

We collected responses on (C)MT-Bench and BFF-Bench from a diverse set of six models across various model families and sizes: Gemma 2 2b [^50], Qwen 2.5 7b [^43], Phi-4 [^1], Yi 1.5 34B [^3], Llama 3.3 70B [^17], and GPT-4o [^39].<sup>4</sup> Each model was evaluated with a temperature of 0.7 using vLLM [^25], resulting in a total of 1,200 question-response pairs.<sup>5</sup>

For each question-response pair, we collected three annotations of correctness. For questions from BFF-Bench, the same financial professionals served as annotators. Annotators received the human-written reference solution and could use external resources except LLM-based chatbots. They were instructed to grade strictly: if any part was wrong, the entire response was marked wrong. Full guidelines are in Section˜A.4. Dataset statistics are in Table˜1.

## 4 Experimental Setup

We experiment on two judgment tasks derived from our VERDICTS dataset, measuring agreement with human annotators for both the standard LLM-as-a-Judge pipeline and a collection of baselines. The final dataset composition is in Table˜1.

### 4.1 Judgment Tasks

For the single grading task, the judge is given a question-response pair and must classify it as “Correct” or “Incorrect”. For the pairwise grading task, the judge is given a question and two responses and must determine which is correct. We ensure that each pair has one response labeled “Correct” and one labeled “Incorrect” by the annotators. Despite not reflecting how judges are used in practice, we do this to provide a performance metric that can be fairly compared to the single grading task.

We evaluate both tasks using Cohen’s $\kappa$ [^12]; an alternative version of the results using F1 are in Table˜5. More details on metrics and confidence intervals are in Appendix˜D.

### 4.2 LLM-as-a-Judge

Our judges consist of the same set of six models used as candidates. The only exception is Gemma 2 2B, which was used only as a candidate.<sup>6</sup> The judge model is allowed to use Chain-Of-Thought [^59] to first generate a set of reasoning steps before generating a final answer. To reduce the overall variance in this analysis, we compute our judgments using self-consistency [^55], taking the majority vote over five separate inferences generated with a temperature of 0.7.

For the single grading case, the model is prompted to grade only the correctness of the model response. We include the full interaction history for the multi-turn questions within the judgment prompt. The model receives a similar prompt for the pairwise grading case, but with instructions to select the more correct response of the two provided. Following [^64], we run each pair twice, reversing the ordering of the references so as to account for any positional bias the judge model may have (e.g., always favoring the first model’s response). If the judge model’s selection differs between the two runs, we count it as a tie.

We use a version of the MT-Bench prompts modified to focus on correctness, which can be found in Appendix˜B. To ensure our results are robust to the underlying prompt, we re-run our single grading experiments with a separate prompt that contains a rephrasing of the same instructions. These results are discussed in Section˜A.6, but we find no meaningful variation.

### 4.3 Grading References

Our main analysis concerns the impact of the provided reference on the judge’s performance. Specifically, we look at three main types of provided references: (1) None: no reference is provided within the LLM Judge’s prompt, (2) Self: the LLM Judge provides its own reference answer and (3) Human: a human provides a manually created reference answer. In the None case, the model is prompted to directly evaluate the provided response without a reference. For all other cases the prompt is identical except for the text of the reference.

In addition, we evaluate three types of baseline reference. Wrong refers to human-edited versions of the Human references that try to maintain the overall style but are incorrect. Each has some amount of inaccurate information or incorrect reasoning that changes the final answer to the question. Any correct response would then be incorrect with respect to these references. However, it would not impact the grading of responses that were already incorrect unless they happened to make the same error. Random are randomly selected references from other questions. These references are non-informative without being actively misleading.

### 4.4 Baselines

We evaluate three automated grading baseline methods of response quality: lexical overlap metrics, embedding models, and reward models. Because these methods output quality scores, we focus our baseline evaluation on pairwise grading, labeling the response with the higher score as correct. The lexical overlap metrics capture how well-designed heuristics can perform on this task. We score each potential response with respect to the gold human-written reference. We evaluate BLEU [^42], ROUGE [^31], and METEOR [^6]. We convert an embedding model into a quality score by computing the cosine similarity between each response and the human-written reference. We select five high-performing, open-weight embedding models on the English split of MTEB [^38].<sup>7</sup> Reward models are LLM-based models explicitly trained to predict human preferences and are increasingly used within alignment pipelines. We compute the reward for each response directly, and select the response with the highest reward as being the “Correct” response. We select the five highest performing reward models on RewardBench [^26].

The full list of the baselines evaluated and their performance can be found in Appendix˜F.

## 6 Analysis

#### LLM-as-a-Judge with human-written references leads to the highest agreement with the human annotators.

Overall, providing a human-written reference improves judges’ agreement with human annotators for all evaluated judges across both the single and pairwise cases. Notably, providing a human reference to a relatively small model (such as Qwen 2.5 7B) can yield better judgments than using a larger model without human references (such as GPT-4o) on the same set of responses.

#### Without a human-written reference, LLM Judges only achieve high agreement on questions they were able to answer as candidates.

For each question in the evaluation, we have a label indicating whether the judge model was able to answer that question correctly when used as a candidate model.<sup>8</sup> When we condition the judge’s agreement on this label in Table˜4, there are clear differences in judgment performance. For questions the judge model was unable to correctly answer, there are large differences in the agreement between using different reference types. This is especially the case for higher-performing judges. For example, when GPT-4o uses its own responses to questions as a reference (i.e., Self response), the Cohen’s $\kappa$ decreases from 0.86 to 0.16 in the pairwise case on questions GPT-4o could not answer. On questions the judge had answered correctly, providing human references still improves performance. However, while these differences are meaningful, they are much lower than when the model was incorrect. This is largely due to the higher judgment performance on correct instances.

#### Different types of reference lead to different profiles of judge behavior.

One common judgment bias is self-preference [^41], in which a model tends to overrate its own responses. To quantify this bias, we analyzed the different types of errors the LLM Judges were making. In this context, the false positive rate (FPR) represents the rate at which the model erroneously labels incorrect responses as being correct and the false negative rate (FNR) represents the rate at which the model erroneously labels correct responses as incorrect. A model with a strong self-preference bias would exhibit a higher FPR and a lower FNR when grading its own responses compared to when grading responses from other models.

Figure˜4 displays the FPR and FNR aggregated over each judge with the Wrong, Random, None, Self, and Human reference types on the single grading task.<sup>9</sup> For every reference type, we see that on average models have a higher FPR when grading their own responses. The gap is particularly large when the model is provided with its own generated reference or no reference at all. Providing a human reference reduces both the overall rate of both error types and the relative difference between a model’s judgment of its own responses and those of others.

When the model is given a Wrong reference, the FPR is low and the FNR is high, which suggests that the incorrect information contained in the reference leads judges to erroneously classify correct responses as incorrect.<sup>10</sup> However, this effect is not observed with the Random reference. This evidence suggests that judges are sensitive to the correctness of the reference, and providing a slightly incorrect reference can in some cases be worse than providing a completely unrelated reference or no reference at all.

Even when provided with a Wrong reference that should make every other response incorrect, LLM Judges were still judging responses to be correct. We analyzed these judgments to understand why this occurred. Although most were simply reasoning errors (e.g. claiming the reference and the response agreed when they did not), we observed cases of a judge model identifying the reference as being incorrect and “overruling” the reference to correctly grade the response. An example of this “overruling” behavior is provided in Appendix˜G.

#### Reference correctness matters more than being human-written.

To better understand what makes human-written references lead to higher agreement with our human annotators, we compared performance across human-written gold references, a set of GPT-4o references judged by humans to be correct, a set of Wrong references, and a set of Random references. Table˜3 presents results for these reference types across all five judges. The disparity observed in the previous section between human-written and synthetic references diminishes. There is also a significant gap between the Human references and the Wrong references. This suggests that the most important part of human-written references is their correctness, rather than any other superficial or stylistic attributes.

## 7 Discussion

We release BFF-Bench and VERDICTS, two new datasets aimed at evaluating how well LLM-as-a-Judge grades response correctness in high-stakes domains. These datasets enable the study of model performance both as candidates and as judges on difficult questions that require deep business and finance domain knowledge.

Using LLM-as-a-Judge does not alleviate the need for human oversight. Our results demonstrate that LLM-as-a-Judge can lead to high agreement when: the LLM Judge (1) can already answer the underlying question or (2) is provided with a correct reference. Having confidence in a judge model requires meeting at least one of these two conditions.

LLM-as-a-Judge performance is sensitive to the provided reference. We observe large variations in performance when changing only the provided reference in the judgment prompt. Providing accurate references increases agreement with human annotators, while providing slightly incorrect references can be worse than providing no reference at all. The disparity in agreement is most pronounced for benchmark questions the judge model answers incorrectly. Improving agreement on such questions is key to advancing the reliability of evaluations of frontier models when using the LLM-as-a-Judge framework.

Therefore, we strongly recommend practitioners (at minimum) verify their reference responses. The results in Table˜3 indicate that verifying responses generated by a model rather than manually writing and checking answers via annotators is sufficient. Although this verification process still requires human effort, our results suggest that this is unavoidable for developing trustworthy LLM-as-a-Judge pipelines. However, note that although using verified references may improve the overall agreement metrics, it may come at the cost of increased bias. Specifically, using a model’s own references exacerbates the self-preference bias compared to providing no reference at all. We leave a more in-depth analysis of the potential pitfalls of using verified, synthetic references to future work.

## Appendix A Annotator Details

All of our datasets relied on two main pools of annotators: domain experts and the authors. The domain expert pool was responsible for:

1. The creation of the original set of BFF-Bench questions.
2. Grading the model outputs for the BFF-Bench questions.
3. Grading the model outputs for the (C)MT-Bench questions.

The authors:

1. Graded the model outputs for the (C)MT-Bench questions.

Our domain expert pool consisted of 9 salaried analysts employed full-time at a financial services company. Each had at least a bachelor’s degree in a business or finance discipline. They were based in the United States and Pakistan. They were not compensated beyond their salary for any work performed for the creation of this dataset.

This work did not present any meaningful risk for our annotators. They were volunteers who accepted the work as part of their normal full-time duties. We were in frequent contact to ensure that they understood the goal of the work and its potential impacts.

### A.1 BFF-Bench

The annotation process occurred in two main stages: dataset creation and model response grading. To create the dataset we first worked with the annotators to develop a set of initial questions and draft responses. We filtered these responses to find questions that were unambiguous, difficult, and self-contained.

Once we had identified a set of questions to use as the base, we then verified the responses. A subset of the annotators were tasked with reviewing each response for accuracy. If a potential error was found, the annotator would propose a new potential response. This proposed response would then go through the same verification process.

We continued this process until we reached 80 two-turn questions with verified answers.

### A.2 (C)MT-Bench

(C)MT-Bench was created after our initial analysis into the references in the MT-Bench dataset. We started by manually going through each provided reference in the math and reasoning category and marking them correct or incorrect. We (the authors) then fixed each error by hand, resulting in corrected reference responses.

### A.3 Grading

The grading process for both datasets occurred within the same user interface. The annotators were given the reference response to the question as a guide and were asked to select whether the model’s response was “Correct”, “Incorrect”, or “Not sure”. There was an additional “Notes” free response box that they could use to flag any issues that arose. Because the references had been verified, we recommended that the annotators generally err on the side of trusting them.

The exact guidelines provided to the annotators are provided in the following section (Section˜A.4).

### A.4 Annotator Guidelines

The model will attempt to answer the provided question (first text box above the other two boxes). A model will generally attempt to fool the judge with confident wrong answers in cases where it does not know the correct answer. Annotators will utilize the Reference (bottom left text box), which contains the answer to the posed question (top text box).

Model Answers should include the relevant facts/formulas needed to answer the question, with a step-by-step explanation of any mathematical manipulations.

It is important to remember that everything should be graded only with respect to the reference answer. If you find any errors within the reference, please mark the error in the notes! However, you should always enter the grade as if the reference is correct.

A general set of guidelines is:

1. If the model’s final answer or recommendation disagrees with the reference, it should be marked “Incorrect”.
2. If the model agrees with the reference but its intermediate steps disagree, it should also be marked “Incorrect”.
3. If the model agrees with the reference, all of the intermediate steps agree, and it provides the additional information that is not obviously wrong based on the reference, it should be marked “Correct”
4. If the model agrees with the reference, all of the intermediate steps agree, but the answer is slightly off due to rounding or performing approximate calculations, it should be marked as “Correct”
5. If the model agrees with the reference, all of the intermediate steps agree, but it is lacking some amount of information given within the reference, you should make a judgment call as to whether the missing information is crucial to the answer. If you are unsure, lean towards “Correct” or mark it “Unsure”

## Appendix D Confidence Intervals on Agreement

To evaluate the agreement between the judges and the human annotators’ consensus, we use the Cohen’s $\kappa$ metric [^12]. This is an agreement measure for categorical data labeled by two raters that accounts for the chance of random agreement. It is defined as:

$$
\kappa=1-\frac{1-p_{o}}{1-p_{e}},
$$

where $p_{o}$ is the observed agreement and $p_{e}$ is the expected chance agreement. $\kappa$ is bounded between -1 and 1, with -1 corresponding to no agreement, 0 corresponding to random agreement and 1 corresponding to total agreement.

For simplicity, we used a boostrap to compute confidence intervals for each of our metrics. Although the intervals that we compute are non-symmetric, we convert them into symmetric intervals for a more compact display in tables. To do so, we select whichever bound leads to the widest interval.

## Appendix G Judge Refusal

The following example is GPT-4o grading a response from Phi-4 using a wrong reference which inverts the definition of annuities and IRAs. The text generated by GPT-4o notes the inconsistencies between the reference and the response, but overrules the reference and still marks the response as correct. We observed this behavior multiple times, only with GPT-4o.  
  
Judgment:

The assistant’s answer provides a breakdown of the differences between annuities and IRAs, similar to the reference answer. Let’s compare the key elements:

1\. Annuity: - The assistant correctly identifies annuities as financial products designed to provide a steady income stream, and they can be immediate or deferred. It mentions different types of annuities (fixed, variable, indexed) and notes the tax-deferred nature of annuities and the penalties for early withdrawal. - The reference answer describes annuities as investment accounts for holding financial assets, emphasizing accumulating contributions and interest until retirement age, and mentions custodial fees and mutual funds’ expense ratios. - There is a discrepancy in the definition: the reference answer incorrectly describes annuities as accounts for holding investments, which aligns more with the description of IRAs.

2\. IRA: - The assistant correctly describes IRAs as tax-advantaged savings accounts with different types (Traditional and Roth) and their unique tax implications. It covers tax treatment, access to funds, and ownership. - The reference answer incorrectly labels IRAs as insurance products, which is inaccurate. It also incorrectly suggests IRAs do not have contribution limits, which is a mistake.

3\. Key Differences: - The assistant highlights investment control, flexibility, tax treatment, and income stream as key differences, which are generally accurate and align with the correct understanding of annuities and IRAs.

Overall, the reference answer contains significant inaccuracies regarding the fundamental nature of annuities and IRAs. In contrast, the assistant’s answer provides a more accurate description and comparison of these financial products.
